# Serenity Research Posts Style Guide

This reference distills four public Serenity posts supplied by the user. It captures reusable structure and reasoning patterns, not a license to impersonate or copy the author.

## Source-Derived Archetypes

### Hidden-bottleneck trade thesis

Use this when a new AI or hardware behavior creates demand for an overlooked listed beneficiary.

Shape:

1. Start with a direct hook: "Fun trade idea", "Interesting bottleneck", "Feels like markets missed this".
2. Name the asset and reason in the first lines.
3. Contrast a popular obvious beneficiary with a smaller, more material one.
4. Explain the technical mechanism causing demand.
5. Show why ordinary alternatives do not fully substitute for it.
6. Add financial anchors and consensus expectations.
7. List downside risks plainly.
8. End with a TLDR.

Typical evidence:

- product hoarding or unusual buying behavior
- AI agents, local inference, orchestration, memory, photonics, CPO, accelerators, networking, storage, or power bottlenecks
- small market cap or revenue base where incremental demand matters
- low social chatter or analyst neglect
- segment revenue share that can re-rate estimates

### Research-method proof

Use this when explaining why a thesis was unusually strong or differentiated.

Shape:

1. Open with confidence, then thank or credit the person who noticed.
2. List the non-obvious pieces of work in arrows or bullets.
3. Combine macro, micro, and technical/derivative angles.
4. Explain the simple takeaway after the complex mapping.
5. Close with why the edge was hard to replicate.

### Credibility / social proof post

Use this for media coverage, third-party validation, or milestones.

Shape:

1. Lead with gratitude.
2. Name the credible outlet, person, or achievement.
3. Mention the thesis components that were covered accurately.
4. Keep it short and sincere.

### Milestone post

Use this for a platform or audience achievement.

Shape:

1. One-line statement of the milestone.
2. Brief comparison or context.
3. Thank the audience.

## Voice Principles

- High-conviction but still probabilistic.
- Casual, not institutional.
- Ticker-dense and mechanism-dense.
- Strong preference for "why this matters now".
- Short paragraphs create speed and emphasis.
- Bullets are used for financials, risks, and technical steps.
- The strongest posts combine anecdotal demand with balance-sheet or estimate materiality.

Avoid:

- pretending to be Serenity
- copying distinctive long passages
- turning the post into generic newsletter prose
- using certainty when the evidence is only anecdotal
- omitting risks in trade ideas
- inventing exact financial data

## Thesis Checklist

Before drafting, answer as many of these as possible:

- What is the new behavior or demand signal?
- Who is buying, hoarding, deploying, or routing around a bottleneck?
- Which company, ETF, option chain, commodity, or supply-chain node benefits?
- Why is the obvious beneficiary less interesting than the overlooked one?
- Is the incremental demand material relative to the company's size?
- What evidence suggests the market has not priced it in?
- What technical reason makes the demand durable or non-trivial?
- What are the component-cost, capacity, governance, ownership, or adoption risks?
- What would disconfirm the thesis?

## Trade-Idea Template

Use this as a flexible scaffold, not a rigid fill-in form:

```text
Interesting / fun trade idea: [direction] [ticker]

Reason: [one-line catalyst]

[Popular obvious thing] is getting all the attention, but [overlooked beneficiary] is only [size / market cap / revenue base], so incremental demand is more material.

The mechanism is:

- [technical mechanism 1]
- [technical mechanism 2]
- [why substitutes are imperfect]

Feels like markets have not priced this because [low mentions / stale estimates / wrong category / weird pass-through].

Numbers:

- Revenue: [value]
- Gross profit / margin: [value]
- Net cash / debt: [value]
- Consensus growth: [value]
- Upside case if demand persists: [value]

Main downside risks:

- [risk 1]
- [risk 2]
- [risk 3]

TLDR: [one-sentence thesis]
```

## Research-Method Template

```text
This was strong research because it connected pieces most people kept separate:

-> [macro or demand-cycle observation]
-> [company / supply-chain mapping]
-> [instrument, ETF, option-chain, or volatility detail]
-> [simple investable conclusion]

The edge was not just being directionally right on [theme].

It was finding where the pricing, pass-through, or volatility assumptions were wrong.
```

## Editing Checklist

Use this when critiquing a draft:

- Does the first line state the asset, catalyst, or achievement?
- Is there a clear reason the thesis matters now?
- Are the financial numbers relevant to materiality?
- Are mechanisms specific enough for a technical reader?
- Does the draft distinguish facts from inference?
- Are risks included before the TLDR?
- Is the TLDR simple enough to quote?
- Is any line too close to the source posts or pretending to be Serenity?
